Hermes I

Overview

Mission
Summary

Hermes I is Ad Astra's primary competition vehicle for the Team America Rocketry Challenge (TARC), the nation's largest student rocketry competition. The rocket must reach exactly 800 feet of apogee, carry a raw egg payload, and return it to the ground uncracked within a target flight time window.

The airframe uses a fiberglass layup over a foam mandrel, chosen for its combination of high stiffness and low weight. The fin can is carbon fiber reinforced to maintain stability under the aerodynamic loads expected at TARC flight velocities.

Design Process

Development
Phases

Phase 01
Requirements & Simulation
Defined TARC scoring constraints. Built baseline OpenRocket model and ran Monte Carlo dispersions via AeroViz to identify optimal motor and ballast configuration for a consistent 800ft apogee across varied weather conditions.
Phase 02
Airframe Fabrication
Laid up fiberglass over foam mandrel for the main body tube. Carbon fiber fin can fabricated separately and bonded with aerospace-grade epoxy. Centering rings machined from plywood.
Phase 03
Recovery System Design
Designed dual-deployment system: drogue parachute deploys at apogee for a high-speed descent, main parachute deploys at 400ft for soft landing. Ejection charges sized based on bay volume calculations.
Phase 04 — Current
Egg Payload Integration
Developing egg protection capsule with foam crush zones and a secure mounting interface. Testing G-load survivability for the egg across simulated landing profiles.
Phase 05 — Upcoming
Test Flights & Tuning
Conduct minimum of two qualification flights with egg payload before TARC qualifying event. Document each flight for post-flight analysis review.

Notes

Lessons &
Observations

Early simulations revealed that achieving a consistent 800ft apogee requires tight motor selection tolerances — a difference of half a grain of propellant results in a ~30ft apogee variation. This pushed the team to focus heavily on the Monte Carlo dispersion analysis tools in AeroViz.

The carbon fiber fin can fabrication proved more complex than anticipated. First-attempt fins showed delamination at the root fillet joint, requiring a revised epoxy schedule and clamp technique. This was resolved on the second attempt.
